Law: The Customs (Miscellaneous Amendments) Regulations 2025

This legislation tweaks several customs and postal rules to smooth out processes for importing and exporting goods. Key changes include: introducing a £1,000 penalty for refusing to move goods for customs checks, letting certain foreign postal operators use simpler customs forms, and clarifying when oral or conduct-based customs declarations can be made for temporary imports (like artwork). It also adjusts how goods are valued for customs duty, especially for defective or short-delivered items, and simplifies transit procedures for air and rail shipments. Postal operators get new authorisation rules, and temporary import periods for artworks can now last up to 48 months (with possible extensions). Some paper-based transit procedures are scrapped, and transitional arrangements let foreign postal operators keep working while awaiting formal approval. Most details will be in publicly available HMRC notices.

25 June 2025

Law: The Reporting Cryptoasset Service Providers (Due Diligence and Reporting Requirements) Regulations 2025

These new rules require UK-based crypto platforms and services that exchange crypto for regular money (or trade between different cryptos) to check their users' identities and report certain customer information to HMRC every year. They must also tell users that their details will be shared with tax authorities and keep records for five years. Users must provide confirmation of their details when asked. If crypto businesses don’t follow these rules, they could face penalties unless they have a good excuse. HMRC can ask for information if they suspect a business is covered by these rules, and businesses must register with HMRC if they aren’t already reporting elsewhere. There are also rules about appealing penalties and measures to stop people trying to avoid these requirements.

25 June 2025

Law: The International Tax Compliance (Amendment) Regulations 2025

This update changes UK tax reporting rules to align with new international standards, particularly for crypto assets and financial data sharing. Starting in 2026, financial institutions must follow updated reporting requirements, with some deadlines extended for certain account types. Banks and other financial bodies must register with HMRC and follow stricter rules when gathering customer tax information. The rules also introduce penalties for failing to provide required tax details or register properly, while removing outdated requirements. These changes aim to improve transparency and crack down on tax avoidance by aligning with global agreements.

25 June 2025

Law: The Child Trust Funds (Amendment) Regulations 2025

These rules update the Child Trust Fund rules to allow certain investments (specifically UCITS fund units or shares) to qualify as valid investments until 31 December 2026. After that date, they'll no longer qualify. The change takes effect from 1 October 2024, and more details will be available in a government tax note online.

25 June 2025

Law: The Individual Savings Account (Amendment) Regulations 2025

These rules update ISA regulations to clarify a few key points: they define what a "long-term asset fund" is, set conditions for opening an adult ISA (requiring National Insurance info and ensuring eligibility rules are met), and specify that withdrawn money can only be replaced back into the same ISA account. Investments in certain funds remain valid until 2026, after which rules change. Long-term asset funds are now included as eligible investments for innovative finance ISAs. More details will be available in a government tax note online.

25 June 2025

Law: The Victims and Prisoners Act 2024 (Commencement No. 7) Regulations 2025

Starting June 25, 2025, victims of violent, sexual, or terrorism offenders who are held in psychiatric hospitals under mental health orders will get new rights to share how the crime affected them. They can submit a statement to the mental health tribunal and request to read it at a hearing, unless there’s a good reason to refuse them. The tribunal can consider the statement when deciding release conditions—but not when deciding if the offender should be discharged. Separately, the law also introduces a permanent advocate for victims of major incidents, like disasters, to represent their interests and advise the government. The advocate will report yearly and can also provide updates on specific incidents when needed. These changes were already set out in the Victims and Prisoners Act 2024, and the government doesn’t expect them to cause major impacts for businesses or public services.

Law: The National Health Service (General Medical Services Contracts and Personal Medical Services Agreements) (Amendment) Regulations 2025

These changes update the rules for how general medical services (GMS) and personal medical services (PMS) contracts work in England’s NHS. They tweak existing regulations to cover services provided under these contracts and clarify the requirements when partnerships (like GP practices run by multiple doctors) make changes to who’s involved. Essentially, it’s a technical update to keep the system running smoothly.

25 June 2025

Law: The Water (Special Measures) Act 2025 (Commencement No. 1) Regulations 2025

Starting 23rd June 2025, water companies in England must create and publish yearly plans to reduce pollution incidents, along with progress reports showing how they’re putting those plans into action. These rules also require sewerage companies to include nature-based solutions in their drainage and sewage management plans. Companies that don’t follow these rules could face penalties. The government has already assessed the broader impact of these changes under the 2025 Act, but these specific regulations won’t directly affect businesses or the public sector. More details are available from the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs.

Law: The Investigatory Powers (Amendment) Act 2024 (Commencement No. 3) Regulations 2025

This set of rules puts the final two parts of the 2024 Investigatory Powers Amendment Act into effect. One part deals with the Secretary of State’s power to review certain notices, and the other involves notifying telecom companies about upcoming changes that might affect them. The government believes these changes won’t have a major impact on businesses or organizations.
